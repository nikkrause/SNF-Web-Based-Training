SNF Consolidated Billing

Lesson 1: SNF Background Information

Lesson 1: Introduction and Learning Objectives

In this lesson, you’ll learn about skilled care and Medicare coverage of skilled care. It should take about 10 minutes to complete.

After completing this lesson, you should be able to correctly:

  • Identify Skilled Nursing Facility (SNF) coverage and payment guidelines
  • Recognize why Congress enacted Consolidated Billing (CB)

What Is Consolidated Billing?

Payment for the majority of services provided to beneficiaries in a Medicare Part A-covered SNF stay, including most services provided by entities other than the SNF, is included in a bundled prospective payment made through the Medicare Administrative Contractor (MAC) to the SNF. The SNF must bill these bundled services to the MAC in a consolidated bill. The entity looks to the SNF for payment for services subject to CB and provided by entities other than the SNF.

What Is Skilled Care?

Medicare Part A covers skilled care in a Medicare-certified SNF. Skilled care is nursing or other rehabilitative services, furnished according to physician orders, that:

  • Require the skills of qualified technical or professional health personnel, such as registered nurses, licensed practical (vocational) nurses, physical therapists, occupational therapists, and speech-language pathologists or audiologists
  • Must be provided directly by or under the general supervision of skilled nursing or skilled rehabilitation personnel to assure patient safety and achieve the medically desired result
    • “General supervision” requires initial direction and periodic inspection of the actual activity; however, the supervisor need not always be physically present or on the premises when the assistant performs services

A service is considered skilled care if the inherent complexity of it can only be performed safely and/or effectively by, or under the general supervision of, skilled nursing or skilled rehabilitation personnel.

Skilled care may be necessary to:

  • Improve a patient’s current condition
  • Maintain the patient’s current condition
  • Prevent or slow further deterioration of the patient’s condition

Not All Care Provided to Patients in a SNF Is Skilled Care

A service is not necessarily a skilled nursing service because it is performed by, or under the direct supervision of, a qualified health care professional. If an unskilled person can safely and effectively perform (or self-administer) the service, it is not a skilled nursing service. If the patient’s condition requires the presence of a skilled professional to supervise unskilled nursing services, these services may be skilled care. Always document fully the need for a skilled professional in these situations.

Skilled Care Services

  • Usually needed for short periods of time following hospitalizations
  • Furnished only by professional staff
  • Examples include:
    • Intravenous feeding
    • Skilled physical therapy (PT)
    • Gait training
    • Tube feeding
    • Changing sterile dressings

Unskilled Care Services

  • May be needed for longer periods of time
  • May be furnished by nonprofessional staff
  • Examples include:
    • Custodial care
    • Routine oral medications
    • Supervision of exercises
    • Routine incontinence care
    • Help with activities of daily living like dressing, eating, and bathing

Qualifying for SNF Coverage

A beneficiary must first be eligible for Medicare Part A to qualify for SNF coverage.

Medicare Part A covers a maximum of 100 days of SNF coverage per benefit period. Part B may cover some services rendered to a beneficiary during a covered Part A stay or during a noncovered Part B stay. After a beneficiary exhausts the 100 days of Part A coverage available in a benefit period or drops to a nonskilled level of care, the stay is no longer covered under Part A and is a noncovered Part B stay.

The beneficiary’s status in the SNF (covered or noncovered Part B stay) critically impacts SNF CB.

SNF Coverage Requirements

A beneficiary must meet all the conditions listed on this page and the next page for Medicare Part A to cover the SNF stay:

  • The beneficiary was an inpatient of a hospital for a medically necessary stay of at least 3 consecutive days
  • The beneficiary transferred to the Medicare-certified SNF within 30 days after discharge from the hospital, unless both the following are true:
    • The beneficiary’s condition makes it medically inappropriate to begin an active course of treatment in a SNF immediately after discharge
    • It is medically predictable at the time of the hospital discharge that the beneficiary will require covered care within a predetermined time period, and that prediction is realized

SNF 3-Day Stay Rule Waiver

There is a SNF 3-day waiver for certain SNFs that have a relationship with Accountable Care Organizations (ACOs). For information about the Shared Savings Program (SSP), visit the SSP website. Learn more about the 3-day stay waiver in SSP ACO Qualifying Stay Edits publication.

SNF Coverage Requirements (continued)

  • The beneficiary requires skilled nursing services or skilled rehabilitation services daily. Skilled services must be:
    • Performed by or under the supervision of professional or technical personnel
    • Ordered by a physician
    • Rendered for an ongoing condition for which the beneficiary received inpatient hospital services, or for a new condition that arose during the SNF care for the ongoing condition
    • Necessarily provided on a SNF inpatient basis as a practical matter when considering economy and efficiency
  • The services are reasonable and necessary for diagnosing or treating the beneficiary’s qualifying condition and are of reasonable duration and quantity

SNF Covered Services

The list below includes some of the services commonly used by Medicare Part A residents in a SNF. The SNF must bill the MAC for these services. The SNF pays outside (non-SNF) providers and suppliers. Part A covered services, whether directly provided by the SNF or under arrangement with an outside provider or supplier, include:

  • Nursing services
  • PT, Occupational Therapy (OT), and Speech-Language Pathology (SLP) services
  • Drugs, biologicals, supplies, appliances, and equipment
  • Enteral and parenteral nutrition
  • Labs and X-ray services
  • “Incident to” physician professional services
  • Ambulance services—unless excluded
  • Orthotics
  • Prosthetics—except for certain excluded customized prosthetic devices
  • Services received in an emergency room that are not for emergency treatment
  • Room and board and all ancillary services

Noncovered SNF Facility Stay

When a beneficiary no longer requires a skilled level of care or exhausts his or her 100 days, he or she may still reside in the SNF. This is a noncovered Part B stay. If this occurs:

  • The beneficiary pays for the room and board charges
  • Medicare may pay for certain ancillary services covered under Part B if the beneficiary has Part B coverage
  • The only services still subject to CB are therapy services (that is, PT, OT, and SLP)

Noncovered SNF Facility Stay (continued)

When a beneficiary resides in an uncertified Medicare nursing home (or part of the home):

  • The Medicare Part A extended-care benefit cannot cover the beneficiary’s stay
  • The beneficiary may still be eligible for Part B coverage of certain individual services, including therapy
  • The beneficiary is not considered a SNF resident for Medicare billing purposes
  • The therapy services are not subject to CB, so either the therapy provider or the facility may submit a Part B bill for the services

Billing Frequency

Bill inpatient services in SNFs:

  • Upon discharge of the resident
  • When the resident’s benefits are exhausted
  • When the resident in the SNF ceases to need skilled care
  • Monthly (and if necessary, monthly thereafter)

Submit bills sequentially.

SNF Facility Payment

The Balanced Budget Act (BBA) of 1997 enacted the SNF Prospective Payment System (PPS) reimbursement and CB requirement methods. Under the SNF PPS, Medicare reimburses SNFs on a per diem basis for virtually the entire package of services that it furnishes during a resident’s covered Part A stay. For more information on the SNF PPS, visit the Skilled Nursing Facility PPS webpage.

Medicare pays for SNF Part A care under the SNF PPS. Under the CB provision, SNF Part A inpatient services include all Part A services within the scope or capability of SNFs. In some cases, the SNF must obtain some services it does not provide directly.

Why Did Congress Enact Consolidated Billing?

Prior to the BBA, a SNF could furnish services to a beneficiary in a covered Part A stay:

  • Directly, using its own resources
  • Through the SNF’s transfer agreement with a hospital
  • Under arrangement with an independent therapist for PT, OT, and SLP services

In each of these circumstances, the SNF billed the MAC for services. However, the SNF also had the option of “unbundling” a service altogether. This allowed the SNF to permit an outside provider or supplier to furnish the service directly to the beneficiary, and that outside provider or supplier would submit a bill to the MAC without any involvement from the SNF. This practice created several problems.

Why Did Congress Enact Consolidated Billing? (continued)

Duplicate Billing: Allowing claims involving a single SNF resident to originate from multiple sources (SNF and provider or supplier) increased potential for duplicate billing. Much of this duplicate billing went undetected because the various claims processing systems didn’t communicate well with each other.

Increased Expenses for Beneficiaries: Beneficiaries incurred a cost-sharing liability for the Part B deductible and coinsurance even if a given service was billed only under Part B. This occurred because billing a service to Part B required the beneficiary to make out-of-pocket payments for the Part B coinsurance and any unmet Part B deductible, in addition to the SNF copayments for days 21 through 100 of the covered Part A stay.

Lack of Accountability: A lack of accountability resulted from a variety of outside entities furnishing and billing for services. This reduced the SNF’s own capacity to meet its responsibility to oversee, coordinate, and account for the total package of care furnished to each of its residents.

Based on process problems, Congress enacted the SNF CB requirements in the BBA.

Lesson 1 Summary

In Lesson 1, you’ve learned:

  • Skilled care includes nursing or other rehabilitative services, furnished according to physician orders and requiring the skills of qualified technical or professional health personnel and provided directly by or under the general supervision of these skilled nursing or skilled rehabilitation personnel.
  • Medicare Part A coverage for skilled care requires meeting certain conditions, such as a 3-day qualifying hospital stay (with limited exceptions).
  • When a beneficiary doesn’t meet these conditions or exhausts his or her 100 days of Skilled Nursing Facility (SNF) care per benefit period, the beneficiary may begin a noncovered Part B stay.
  • Medicare pays for SNF Part A care under the SNF Prospective Payment System. Under the Consolidated Billing (CB) provision, SNF Part A inpatient services include all Part A services within the scope or capability of SNFs. In some cases, the SNF must obtain some services it does not provide directly.
  • CB avoids problems with duplicate billing, increased cost-sharing expenses for beneficiaries, and lack of accountability.

Statutory and Regulatory Provisions
Under section 1140 of the Social Security Act (the Act), there are restrictions regarding the use of certain words, letters, emblems and symbols in connection with advertisements, solicitations or other productions. Specifically, the words “Social Security,” “Social Security Account,” “Social Security System,” “Social Security Administration,” “Medicare,” “Health Care Financing Administration,” “Department of Health and Human Services,” “Health and Human Services,” “Supplemental Security Income Program,” or “Medicaid” may not be used in a manner that gives (or could give) the impression that the solicitation, advertisement or other production is endorsed, authorized, affiliated with or approved by the Centers for Medicare and Medicaid Services or by the Department of Health and Human Services.

Similarly, letters such as “SSA,” “HCFA,”[1] “DHHS,” “HHS,” “SSI,” or other combinations or variations also may not be used to imply approval or involvement by the Department of Health and Human Services or CMS. The same rules apply to the use of symbols and emblems associated with the Department of Health and Human Services or with the Centers for Medicare and Medicaid Services, such as the design of the social security card, the Medicare card, or envelopes or stationary used by either entity. State agencies or political subdivisions of state agencies are exempt from these restrictions.

Government publications are not subject to copyright law.[2] However, section 1140(a)(2)(B) of the Act prohibits the reproduction, reprinting or distribution of items consisting of forms, applications or other publications of the Social Security Administration or the Department of Health and Human Services for a fee unless specific, written authorization is obtained as prescribed by regulations published by the Commissioner of Social Security or the Secretary of the Department of Health and Human Services.[3] Violations of Section 1140 are punishable by a civil money penalty of $5,000 for each piece of advertisement, solicitation or other production, and up to $25,000 for each instance where a broadcast or telecast is used as the means of transmission for the solicitation or advertisement. See 42 C.F.R. § 1003.102(b)(7). Section 1140 is enforced by the Office of the Inspector General. It should be noted that the use of a disclaimer to inform the public that a given solicitation or advertisement is not endorsed or approved of by any governmental entity does not waive the requirements of section 1140.

Analysis
CMS information may be used in an advertisement or solicitation if certain words, letters, emblems and symbols are not used in a manner that could conceivably give the impression that the advertisement has been approved, authorized or sanctioned by either the Department of Health and Human Services or CMS. The information CMS makes public on its web site can be incorporated into another web site for without prior authorization if the commercial entity does not charge a fee for reproductions of forms, applications or other government publications. If an individual wishes to copy a CMS publication, form or application verbatim in order to sell it to the public, CMS may refuse to grant written authorization for reproduction. However, it would be permissible for an individual to retrieve information from CMS’ website, incorporate or synthesize that information into his own publication, and sell the publication to the public. Arguably, CMS’ website constitutes a “publication” that cannot simply be copied by an individual who wishes to sell the information.
